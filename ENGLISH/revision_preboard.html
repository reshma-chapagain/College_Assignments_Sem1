Chapter 1 ¬∑ Grammar

Subject-Verb Agreement

Definition: The subject and verb in a sentence must agree in number (singular/plural) and person. A singular subject takes a singular verb; a plural subject takes a plural verb.

Core Rules (Rules 1 10)

Rule 1: Two subjects joined by and ‚Üí plural verb.
You and I are friends.  |  Bed and table are expensive.
Rule 2: Two singular subjects joined by and that express ONE idea ‚Üí singular verb.
Slow and steady wins the race.  |  Rice and curry is my favourite.
Exception: Time and tide wait for no man. (idiomatic)
Rule 3: Singular noun + preposition + plural noun ‚Üí singular verb (goes with first noun).
The water of these streams is clean.
Rule 4: Subjects joined by as well as, with, together with, along with, rather than, in addition to, except, besides, no less than, and not ‚Üí verb agrees with the first subject.
He, as well as they, is running.  |  My friends, as well as I, are reading.
Rule 5: Two/more singular subjects joined by or, either-or, neither-nor ‚Üí singular verb.
Neither he nor she was writing.
Rule 6: If one subject is plural (connected by or/nor) ‚Üí verb is plural and the plural noun is placed closest to the verb.
Either the teacher or his students are coming.
Rule 7: Subjects of different persons connected by or/nor ‚Üí verb agrees with the subject just before it.
Either you or he is tall.  |  Neither he nor they are innocent.
Rule 8 (231 / 123 Rule): Order of singular persons: 2nd, 3rd, 1st (You, he, I). Order of plural: 1st, 2nd, 3rd (We, you, they). In both cases ‚Üí plural verb.
You, he and I are friends.  |  We, you and they are neighbours.
Rule 9: Vague pronouns (someone, everyone, anyone, no one, somebody, everybody, nobody, many a, each, either, neither) ‚Üí always singular verb.
Somebody is hiding.  |  Each of them was happy.
Rule 10: Two nouns joined by and but qualified separately by each/every ‚Üí singular verb.
Each day and each night has its pleasure.

Special Cases (Rules 11 36)

Rule 11: Two nouns joined by and together qualified by each/every ‚Üí singular.
Every man and woman has to die.
Rule 12: Title of book/quotation as subject ‚Üí singular verb.
'Hard Times' is a very interesting novel.
Rule 13: Subject expressing quality, amount, or distance as a single unit ‚Üí singular.
Two kilometers is not a long distance.  |  Five minutes is sufficient.
Rule 14 Plural form, singular meaning: Aesthetics, aeronautics, athletics, ethics, linguistics, news, physics, mathematics, politics, measles, The United States, The United Nations ‚Üí singular verb.
Measles is a dangerous disease.  |  The United States is a big country.
Rule 15 Always plural: Clothes, scissors, spectacles, assets, goods, wages, riches, thanks ‚Üí plural verb.
His wages are Rs. 500.  |  His scissors are not sharp.
Rule 16 Fraction: Verb agrees with the noun after "of".
Two-thirds of the novel is interesting.  |  Two-thirds of the novels are interesting.
Rule 17: A number of ‚Üí plural.  |  The number of ‚Üí singular.
A number of students are playing.  |  The number of students is small.
Rule 18 Collective phrases: A band of, a series of, a team of, a herd of, a set of, a flock of, a class of ‚Üí singular verb.
A band of musicians has come.
Rule 19 Collective nouns: Class, committee, government, jury, army, team, family, etc. ‚Üí singular if denoting a unit; plural if denoting individual members.
The class is going into the hall.  |  The class are debating this point.
Rule 20 Always plural nouns: Cattle, clergy, police, public, people, gentry ‚Üí plural verb.
The police are coming.  |  The cattle are grazing.
Rule 21 Always singular nouns (uncountable): Advice, bread, clothing, furniture, hair, information, luggage, poetry, scenery ‚Üí singular verb.
Your information is based on facts.  |  All my furniture was sold.
Rule 22: Two or more titles/designations of the same person joined by and (no article before second) ‚Üí singular.
The principal and accountant is on leave.
Rule 23: Two or more titles with the before each ‚Üí plural.
The principal and the accountant are on leave.
Rule 24: None of ‚Üí plural verb.
None of the candidates have come.
Rule 25 The + adjective: The blind, the deaf, the old, the poor, the rich, the dead ‚Üí plural verb.
The rich are cruel.  |  The poor are laborious.
Rule 26 Nationality words (-sh, -ch, -ese) with "the": The British, The French, The Nepalese, The Burmese ‚Üí plural verb.
The Nepalese are honest.
Rule 27: More than one + singular noun ‚Üí singular verb.
More than one student is playing.
Rule 28: More + plural noun + than one ‚Üí plural verb.
More books than one are missing.
Rule 29: Singular noun + preposition + same singular noun ‚Üí singular.
Letter after letter has been sent.
Rule 30: All referring to everything ‚Üí singular. Referring to people/things ‚Üí plural.
All is well.  |  All are playing.
Rule 31: One of + plural noun ‚Üí singular verb.
One of the teachers is outside.
Rule 32: Two or more adjectives before a singular noun ‚Üí plural verb.
Social and political freedom are necessary.
Rule 33: One or two ‚Üí plural verb.
One or two boys are outside.
Rule 34: A clause as subject ‚Üí singular verb.
What you are doing is good.
Rule 35: Two clauses as subject ‚Üí plural verb.
What you think and do are praiseworthy.
Rule 36: Nothing but ‚Üí singular verb.
Nothing but trees is seen.

Active Voice & Passive Voice

üü¢ Active Voice

The subject does the action.
She eats an apple.
They read novels.

üîµ Passive Voice

The subject receives the action.
An apple is eaten by her.
Novels are read.

When to use Passive Voice:
(i) Action is more important than the doer  |  (ii) Doer is unknown  |  (iii) Scientific/technical writing  |  (iv) Doer is known to all (America was discovered in 1492.)

General Formula

A.V.:   S + V + O
P.V.:   O(S) + 'be' verb + V3 + by + S(O)

Simple PastS + V2 + OO(S) + was/were + V3 + by + S

Special Rules (Key Ones)

Prepositions instead of "by":
  • know, oblige, marry ‚Üí to  |  Kalidas is known to all.
  • please, displease, satisfy, cover ‚Üí with  |  I am pleased with Sarita.
  • interest, contain, consist ‚Üí in  |  Protein is contained in milk.
  • astonish, surprise, shock ‚Üí at  |  I was shocked at his conduct.
Vague pronouns omitted in PV: You, we, they, one, someone, people, nobody, none ‚Üí omitted in PV (with "not" used for nobody/none).
No one could do that ‚Üí That could not be done.
Imperative sentences:
Order: V1 + obj ‚Üí Let + object + be + V3
Bring a glass of water ‚Üí Let a glass of water be brought.

Please/Kindly: ‚Üí You are requested to + V1
Please give me your pen ‚Üí You are requested to give me your pen.

Advice: V1 + obj ‚Üí O(S) + should be + V3
Love your country ‚Üí Your country should be loved.
That-clause / Reporting verbs (say, think, know, believe, consider, etc.):
Two patterns:
  1. Subject + be + V3 + to-infinitive
    People consider that he is wise ‚Üí He is considered to be wise.
  2. It + be + V3 + that-clause
    ‚Üí It is considered that he is wise.
S + make + agent + bare infinitive:
The joker made us laugh ‚Üí We were made to laugh. (to-infinitive added in PV)
Verbs + prep + object:
I look after him ‚Üí He is looked after (by me).
‚ö†Ô∏è Note: I, we, you, they, people, all, someone, everyone, no one, nobody, somebody, everybody, anybody ‚Üí NOT used after "by" in Passive Voice.

Sequence of Tense / Mixed Use of Tense

Definition: Mixed Use of Tense (also called Sequence of Tense) refers to rules that govern how the tense of the subordinate clause relates to the tense of the principal clause.

Key Rules

Rule 1 Simple Past in main clause: Subordinate clause ‚Üí corresponding past (would/could + V1).
Gita said that she would go to London the next day.
Exception: If subordinate clause states universal truth or present habit ‚Üí Simple Present.
The teacher said that the sun rises in the east.
Rule 2 "since" + Simple Past: Principal clause ‚Üí Present Perfect Tense.
I have known him since he started living here.
Rule 3 "lest": Imperative in main clause ‚Üí subordinate clause uses should + V1.
Labour hard lest you should fail.
Rule 4 Two past actions at intervals: First action ‚Üí Past Perfect; Second action ‚Üí Simple Past.
When the police came, the thieves had already run away.
Rule 5 One past action ongoing when another happened: Ongoing ‚Üí Past Continuous; Interrupting action ‚Üí Simple Past.
She met with an accident while she was crossing the road.
Rule 6 Two simultaneous past actions: Same tense for both.
When he pressed the brakes, the bus stopped.
Rule 7 "as if / as though" (present doubt): Principal ‚Üí Simple Present; Subordinate ‚Üí Simple Past.
She walks as though she were drunk.
Rule 8 "as if / as though" (real past event): Principal ‚Üí Simple Past; Subordinate ‚Üí Past Perfect.
He spoke as though he had not prepared well.
Rule 9 "I wish / If only" (desire): ‚Üí Simple Past or (would/could + V1).
I wish I had a car.  |  If only I could lift that box.
Rule 10 "I wish / If only" (regret): ‚Üí Past Perfect.
I wish I had not left my home.
Rule 11 Two clauses with same subject joined by "and": Same tense (unless time adverbials are given).
He bought a necklace and gave it to his girlfriend.
Rule 12 "than" or "as": Both clauses in same tense.
He drinks much more than his friend does.
Rule 15 Relative clause: Relative clause (who/which/that) and main clause use same tense.
I dislike those boys who tease girls.
Rule 17 "This/It is the first/second time...": ‚Üí Present Perfect Tense.
This is the first time I have ever visited London.
Rule 18 "It's time / It's high time": ‚Üí Simple Past (present meaning).
It's high time we went home.
Rule 19 "It is + period + since": ‚Üí Simple Past or Present Perfect.
It is three years since I saw Bill.
Rule 20 "No sooner...than": No sooner + Past Perfect + than + Simple Past.
No sooner had he reached the station than the train left.
Rule 21 "Hardly...when": Hardly + Past Perfect + when + Simple Past.
Hardly had I corrected one set of papers, when another set arrived.
Rule 22 Simple Present main clause + reporting verbs: Subordinate clause ‚Üí Simple Future.
Verbs: assume, believe, doubt, expect, hope, know, think, wonder, etc.
I think he will sell the house.
Rule 23 "until": Imperative + until + Simple Present Tense.
Heat the water until it boils.
Chapter 4 ¬∑ Writing Skills

Note Making & Summary Writing

Note Making

Definition: A systematic method of writing down quickly, briefly, and clearly the important points of a reading text. The word 'note' means a "brief written record as an aid to memory."

Types of Notes

Concept ordering notes, revision notes, research notes, comparative notes, and analytical notes.

Functions of Note Making

  • Keep a record of main points for future reference
  • Revise for an examination or writing assignment
  • Update information
  • Reinforce or compare information from different sources
  • Analyse a text

Note Making Involves

  • Reading strategy: Careful reading to identify the central idea, main points, and supporting details
  • Note-writing techniques: Topicalising, copying, transcribing, and schematizing
  • Reduction devices: Using abbreviations and symbols
  • Organization techniques
  • Methods of sequencing: Numerals and letters, decimalization

Summary Writing

Definition: A process of condensing information without changing the original meaning and focus of a passage. 'Summary' means "summed up" or "condensed."

Related Terms

TermMeaning
AbstractA very short version of a long text (found in research articles)
SynopsisA brief account of a research plan submitted before the research begins
Pr√©cisA brief account of something without details or formalities (synonymous with summary)
OutlineA sketch in phrases/sentences that professional writers make before writing a document

3 Techniques of Summary Writing

1. Selection

Select the theme, main ideas, and key phrases that support the main ideas.

2. Rejection

Omit: repetitions, examples and illustrations, redundant expressions, minor supporting details.

3. Substitution

Synthesis (combining sentences), sentence substitution, and one-word substitution.

üí° Study Tip: Summary writing is a productive skill integrating both reading and writing skills. A good summary is never more than 1/3 the length of the original text.
Chapter 5 ¬∑ Professional Writing

Memo Writing (Memorandum)

Definition: A memorandum (memo) is the most frequently used form of communication within an organization. It is also called an inter-office memorandum. It is brief, to the point, and flows in all directions: upward, downward, and horizontal.

Objectives of Writing a Memo

  • To make announcements
  • To make requests and suggestions
  • To send instructions or directives
  • To clarify previous messages
  • To provide information
  • To report on the progress of a project
  • To confirm matters that have been discussed
  • To state minor amendments to company policies

3 Parts of a Memorandum

Part 1: The Memohead

  • Name of the organization
  • Heading: MEMORANDUM
  • FROM:  |  TO:  |  REF:  |  DATE:  |  SUBJECT:

Part 2: The Message

The message is paragraphed (fully blocked layout). If making several distinct points, number them for clarity. No complimentary opening required.

Part 3: The Ending

  • Signature
  • cc (carbon copies)
  • Enc. (enclosures)
‚ö†Ô∏è Important: NEVER write "Yours sincerely" at the end of a memo. There is no complimentary close.

Language & Style

Complete sentences are not always necessary. Determiners and auxiliary verbs are sometimes left out. "Dear" and "Sincerely" are not required. The style is concise and direct.

Sample Memo Structure

National Tours and Travels Pvt. Ltd.
                   MEMORANDUM

TO:    Kushal Pathak, Training Organizer
FROM:   Ashok Maharjan, General Manager
REF:    AM/NTTPL
DATE:   September 3, 2012
SUBJECT: SPECIAL TRAINING COURSES ON SALES AND MARKETING

[Message body here...]

___________________
[Signature]
cc: [names]
Enc: [list]
Chapter 6 ¬∑ Professional Writing

Formal Technical Report Writing

Definition: A professional report is a formal communication written for a specific purpose, conveying authentic information to a well-defined audience in a completely impartial and objective manner, and containing recommendations if required. Key qualities: Usefulness, Clarity, and Economy.

Purposes of a Report

  • Presenting data
  • Describing problems and suggesting solutions
  • Discussing and analyzing data
  • Recording events and happenings
  • Analyzing a situation or condition
  • Giving feedback, suggestions, or recommendations

Types of Reports

TypeDescription
InformationalPresents facts without analysis or recommendations
AnalyticalAnalyzes a problem and provides recommendations
FormalResult of thorough investigation; longer, elaborate; fixed format
Non-formal (Informal)Brief; routine; uses printed forms, letter, or memo format
RoutinePeriodic/regular reports (daily, monthly)
SpecialPrepared for specific, one-time situations

4 Formats of Reports

1. Printed Forms

For routine data collection. Simple fill in blanks or tick items. Systematic and objective.

2. Letter Format

For short reports communicated to someone outside the organization.

3. Memo Format

For short reports communicated within the organization. Contains memo elements + additional sections.

4. Manuscript Format

For long and formal reports. Divided into sections and sub-sections with clear headings.

Parts of a Formal Technical Report

A formal report has three divisions:

Front Matter

  1. Cover Page
  2. Title Page
  3. Preface / Letter of Transmittal
  4. Acknowledgements
  5. Table of Contents
  6. List of Figures and Tables
  7. Abstract and Summary

Main Body

  1. Introduction (Background, Purpose, Significance, Scope, Methods, Organization)
  2. Discussion or Description
  3. Conclusions
  4. Recommendations

Back Matter

  1. Appendix
  2. Glossary
  3. List of References
  4. Bibliography
  5. Index
üí° Remember: Informational reports may have no conclusions/recommendations. Not all elements are always required.

Body Section Details

Introduction addresses:

Background information, Purpose of the report, Significance, Scope, Methods and sources, Organization of the report.

Discussion Section Tips:

  • Provide information in the most logical order
  • Provide ample explanation for complex information
  • Use headings and subheadings
  • Introduce graphics and provide explanations
  • Identify each figure with a title and number
  • Place all tables and figures after they are mentioned in the text

References vs Bibliography:

A List of References contains only sources actually cited in the text. A Bibliography is a list of all sources consulted, arranged alphabetically and serially numbered.

Chapter 7 ¬∑ Communication Skills

Group Discussion (GD)

Definition: A Group Discussion is a process where exchange of ideas and opinions are debated upon. A small group is given a topic (general or specific), a time limit, and each participant must share their views. Panelists judge the discussion and select the best candidate.

10 Tips for Group Discussion

#TipKey Point
1Adequate Subject MatterHave in-depth knowledge; be aware of current events locally and globally. Relevant data gives you an edge.
2Read WidelyRegular reading (not last-minute prep) adds to knowledge and vocabulary. Choose content-rich magazines.
3Choose Quality SourcesAvoid advertisement-heavy magazines; choose informative content.
4Know Repeated TopicsTopics like terrorism, gender inequality, poverty, privatization reappear often. Prepare these with facts and dates.
5Work on Communication SkillsGood vocabulary, command over English, and rehearsal with friends before the GD.
6Listen to the Topic CarefullyStructure your points on paper first. Avoid deviating from the topic or making strong statements at the start.
7Maintain Balance in ToneDon't raise your voice in objection. Find common ground rather than dismissing others' views.
8Listening Skills are EssentialLet others speak. Acknowledge the speaker with nods or "yes". Shows you are an active participant.
9Body Gestures are ImportantSit straight; don't lean back, knock the table, or get distracted. Panelists observe your body language.
10Be First and Last to SpeakInitiating gives advantage. Closing/summarizing also earns brownie points. Conclude even if no consensus was reached.
üí° Golden Analogy: "Imagine you are driving you have the power to accelerate or brake. Be that conscientious driver in a GD. Take initiative, know when to brake, and steer towards a conclusion."

What NOT to Do in a GD

  • Do not open with strong statements like "I completely agree/disagree"
  • Do not deviate from the main topic
  • Do not dismiss others' points without acknowledging them
  • Do not speak throughout give others a chance
  • Do not get distracted (e.g., looking at the door when it opens)
  • Do not lean back on the chair or knock the table

In-text Citation APA & MLA

Purpose: Citation is giving credit to the original author when you use their ideas, information, or exact words in your writing. Two main styles: APA (American Psychological Association) and MLA (Modern Language Association).

APA Style

Uses the Author-Year system. Past tense or present perfect tense is used in signal phrases.

1. Author named in a signal phrase

Althen (2003) has contended that people's communication style is "strongly influenced by cultural values" (p. 33).

‚Üí Format: Author (Year) has verb that "quote" (p. page).

2. Author named in parentheses

There is a strong need for educational training (Grisham, 1999).

‚Üí Format: (Author's last name, Year)

3. Online/Web sources

HIV testing is essential in preventing transmission (World Health Organization, 2004).

‚Üí Use organization name if no individual author.

APA Reference List Format (Book)

Author, I. (Year). Title of book in italics (edition). Place: Publisher.

Althen, G. (2003). American ways: A guide for foreigners in the United States (2nd ed.). Yarmouth: Intercultural Press.

APA elements for a book: 1. Author, 2. Year, 3. Title & subtitle, 4. Place, 5. Publisher

MLA Style

Uses the Author-Page system.

1. Author named in a signal phrase

Althen contends that people's communication style is "strongly influenced by cultural values" (33).

‚Üí Format: Author verb that "quote" (page number).

2. Author named in parentheses

There is a strong need for educational training (Grisham 190).

‚Üí Format: (Author's last name Page number) ‚Äî no comma between name and page.

3. Online sources

HIV testing is essential (World Health Organization 1).

MLA Works Cited Format

Author Last, First. Title of Book. Edition. Place: Publisher, Year.

Althen, Gary. American Ways: A Guide for Foreigners in the United States. 2nd ed. Yarmouth: Intercultural P, 2003.

Chapter 9 ¬∑ Professional Writing

Email Writing (Electronic Mail)

Definition: Electronic mail (e-mail) is an effective, low-cost, and instant method of communication. Messages are keyed into a computer workstation and transmitted to recipients. A single message can be sent simultaneously to many recipients.

Evolution of E-mail

Businesses today operate in a highly competitive market where high-speed communication and information transfer are essential. Most office activities are electronic, using computer-based technology. E-mail has evolved as the primary method of workplace communication ‚Äî replacing formal printed memos, faxes, and letters in many situations.

How Email Works (Path of a Message)

1. You prepare the message on your computer.
2. Enter the recipient's email address and click Send.
3. Your message is sent to a server connected to the Internet.
4. The server sends your message to a router.
5. The message travels from router to router until it reaches the correct one.
6. Routers are connected by cables and telephone lines.
7. A router sends your message to the server connected to the recipient's computer.
8. The server reads the address and passes your message to the recipient.

Advantages of E-mail

  • High-speed send/receive cycle
  • Direct input and retrieval from keyboard
  • Virtually instant despatch/retrieval
  • Simultaneous circulation to pre-selected groups
  • Other files can be attached to emails
  • Saves time and effort compared to producing formal printed memos and letters
  • Messages can be retained on an electronic file or printed as hard copy

Parts / Structure of an Email

PartDescription
To:Recipient's email address ‚Äî key it accurately
From:Sender's email address (auto-inserted by the computer)
Date/Time:Automatically inserted by your computer
Subject:Use an appropriate, specific subject heading
Salutation/Greeting:Keep it informal and friendly for informal emails (e.g., "Hi Gita" or "Hello Thomas")
Body/Message:Short sentences, short paragraphs; write in a casual style as if speaking; less formal than other business documents
Closing:Informal emails: no formal closing needed (e.g., "See you soon, Shirley"). Semi-formal: add a signature block.
Signature BlockName, designation, organization, phone, mobile, email, website
DisclaimerOptional; for confidential/professional emails: states the email may be confidential and unauthorized use is prohibited.

Types of Emails: Formal vs Informal

üìß Informal Email
  • Informal greeting (Hi/Hello + first name)
  • Contractions are OK (I'm, you've, I'd)
  • Chatty, casual style
  • No formal closing needed
  • Short and friendly
üì® Semi-formal / Business Email
  • Slightly less informal (Dear + name)
  • Short sentences ‚Äî no padding!
  • Short paragraphs
  • Write as if speaking naturally
  • Standard signature block at end
  • Disclaimer if confidential

Pitfalls / Complaints About Email

  • People write emails too casually and forget basic rules of good business writing
  • Use of text abbreviations (BTW, TTYL, BFN) is inappropriate in business use
  • Emails may be read within seconds of being sent ‚Äî you cannot call them back. Always proofread before clicking send!
  • Lack of respect for the reader ‚Äî spelling, grammar, and formatting errors
  • Misuse of Reply All when only the sender needs to read the response
  • Using email for messages that are too sensitive or complex (should be face-to-face)
‚ö†Ô∏è Golden Rule: "Once your message is sent, it may be read by the recipient within seconds. Have some respect for your reader ‚Äî make sure it is right before you click 'send'."

Oral Presentation

Definition: An oral presentation is a talk or speech in which a new product, idea, or piece of work is shown and explained to a relatively smaller and knowledgeable audience. It is given for specific purposes: class presentations, professional presentations on topics like sales, marketing, production, or proposing a solution to a problem.

Characteristics of a Good Presentation

  • There is a clear structure with an introduction, a discussion, and a conclusion
  • The presenter recognizes and matches the audience's needs, interests, and level of understanding
  • Facts and figures are visually represented in tables, graphs, and charts
  • Humor and anecdotes may be used to create a friendly relationship with the audience (with care)
  • The presenter speaks clearly and logically and uses body language effectively
  • Questions are given serious attention and regarded as an essential part of the presentation

Preparing a Presentation Process

  • Identify the purpose and goal of the presentation
  • Analyze the audience and their needs
  • Collect the relevant information
  • Design and organize the information
  • Time the presentation
  • Decide on the medium of presentation and visual aids
  • Become familiar with the location of the presentation

3-Part Structure of a Presentation

SectionPurposeUseful Phrases
Opening Section Greet, introduce yourself, outline the presentation "The presentation today is divided into three parts. First, I'll... Following that I'll... Finally, I'll..."
Middle Section Introduce sections, expand, use transitions, review earlier points, focus audience "I'd like to start/begin by..." / "Well, we've looked at... Now I'd like to discuss..." / "I'd like to expand on..." / "As I mentioned earlier..."
Concluding Section Summarize, invite questions, close "Finally, let's summarize some of the main points." / "To conclude, I'd like to summarize." / "Does anyone have any questions or comments before we conclude today?"

Delivering the Presentation 5 Key Points

1. Do Not Read the Presentation: A presentation is meant to be an oral, face-to-face communication. Reading a script or slides is possibly the worst thing a presenter can do ‚Äî it fails to grab attention and disrespects the audience.
2. Preview the Presentation: Give a short glance at how the presentation has been organized. Signal to the audience what will come in the beginning, middle, and end. This helps the audience understand the structure.
3. Use Transitions in Language: Using transition words such as first, in addition to, what is more, we can think of, the next step, another important aspect helps the audience follow the direction of the presentation and know when you are shifting to a new idea.
4. Modulate Your Voice: Change the pitch, volume, and intonation according to context. Speaking louder when emphasizing an idea is effective. Appropriate pauses give audiences time to receive and process information.
5. Interact with the Audience: To arouse and sustain audience interest, interact directly or indirectly. To maintain ongoing communication, avoid things that reduce audience involvement ‚Äî diverting from topic, stopping to speak, asking for something constantly.

Rehearsal Why It Matters

Rehearsal helps to:

  • Coordinate speech with visual aids
  • Know if information has been properly edited
  • Check if the duration of the presentation is appropriate
  • Minimize anxiety and fear

Handling Tough Questions

SituationWhat to Say

Inviting more questions"Does anyone have any questions or comments?" / "If you'd like me to elaborate or clarify anything, please ask."
Closing the presentation"If there are no further questions, I'd like to thank you very much for your attention. Please feel free to contact me."
